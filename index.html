Why massage matters

Massage during shampooing helps cleanse effectively, improves client comfort, supports relaxation, and can stimulate the scalp when performed correctly.

Rationale: Good massage is part of the service experience, not just an add-on.

Important rule

Massage should be adapted to the client’s scalp condition, hair type, sensitivity, and service goal.

Do not use vigorous massage on irritated, inflamed, infected, or damaged scalps.

Professional standard

Check comfort, pressure and water temperature before and during the massage. Use the pads of the fingers, not the nails.

1. Effleurage

What it is: light, gliding, soothing movements.

Why used: relaxes the client, spreads product, and begins/ends the massage smoothly.

Best for: most hair types, especially normal, dry, or sensitive scalps.

2. Petrissage

What it is: gentle kneading and lifting movements.

Why used: helps stimulate circulation and loosen tension.

Best for: normal or slightly oily healthy scalps.

3. Friction

What it is: small circular rubbing movements using finger pads.

Why used: helps lift oil, product residue and flakes during cleansing.

Best for: oily scalps or areas needing extra cleansing focus.

4. Tapotement

What it is: light tapping or percussion movements.

Why used: can create a stimulating finish, but used lightly and selectively.

Best for: healthy scalps only; less common in beginner routines.

How to perform safely

Step 1: Check water temperature, comfort and scalp condition.
Step 2: Support the head and neck properly.
Step 3: Use finger pads — never scratch with nails.
Step 4: Start light, then increase only if the client is comfortable.
Step 5: Keep movements even, controlled and symmetrical.
Step 6: Reduce pressure again before finishing.

Technique-by-technique tips

Effleurage

Use both hands to glide smoothly across the scalp. Ideal at the start and end of the shampoo.

Petrissage

Knead gently in sections. Avoid harsh pulling. Best when the scalp is healthy and relaxed pressure is welcome.

Friction

Use small circular movements with finger pads. Focus on oily or congested areas.

Tapotement

Use very light tapping only. Avoid on sensitive or irritated scalps and use sparingly.

Normal hair / scalp

Use: Effleurage + gentle petrissage.

Rationale: supports comfort, circulation and a balanced cleansing routine.

Avoid: overly harsh friction or excessive stimulation.

Oily scalp

Use: Effleurage + friction.

Rationale: friction helps loosen oil and residue while effleurage keeps the service comfortable.

Avoid: very heavy manipulation at the roots.

Dry / sensitive scalp

Use: Light effleurage only, possibly very gentle petrissage if appropriate.

Rationale: soothing techniques reduce discomfort and avoid over-stimulating a dry scalp.

Avoid: vigorous friction and strong tapotement.

Dandruff / flaking scalp

Use: Gentle effleurage, careful friction only if the scalp is not inflamed and product instructions allow.

Rationale: remove loosened flakes gently without aggravating the scalp.

Avoid: aggressive rubbing, scratching or heavy tapotement.

Extra apprentice tips

  • Ask the client about massage pressure preference.
  • Never massage over cuts, infections, open skin or severe irritation.
  • Keep elbows relaxed and posture correct to avoid straining yourself.
  • Work systematically so all areas of the scalp are treated evenly.

Useful knowledge recap

Best order in a shampoo massage

Start with gentle effleurage, introduce more focused technique if appropriate, then reduce pressure and finish calmly.

Why rationale matters

Choosing the right technique for the right scalp condition improves comfort, cleansing quality and professionalism.

When to stop

If the client reports pain, burning, dizziness or discomfort, stop immediately and reassess.
